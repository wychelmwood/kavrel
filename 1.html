You may notice that both /ɾ/ and /ɹ/ are romanized as "r," so what's the difference? Well, if you see "r" at the start or end of a syllable (e.g. "er" (zero) or "rodz" (shelter)), it's pronounced as the latter (voiced alveolar approximant). Otherwise, it's the former (a voiced alveolar tap, e.g. "arze" (ego, hubris))

As you can see, there is no romanization for /ə/. This is because it only has one use case throughout the langauge: if you come across the consonants "zd" in a single syllable, instead of being pronounced as /zd/, we use /zd̪ə/ instead, almost like "zduh" (if you're not familiar with the IPA, the diacritic on /d/ means that it's said with your tongue touching your upper teeth).

Grammar

Kavrėl uses free word order, meaning that grammatical case is used in a way so that any word can be placed anywhere in a sentence (with the exception of particles, but we'll get to that later on).

This means: 

        "The man (nom.) saw the rat (acc.)"
        "The rat (acc.) saw the man (nom.)"
        "The man (nom.) the rat (acc.) saw"

all mean the same thing semantically. With that said, the commonly used word order is Subject-Object-Verb (the man (nom.) the rat (acc.) saw), but it's up to you!

Examples

ta etvy ta kyraza ilokynen.
NP rat-NOM NP sky-ACC 3SG-watch-GNO-FAC-PRES
The rat the sky watches.
"(In general,) the rat watches the sky."
ta lydz ta tynaza ilkyzanathe.
NP water-NOM NP fire-ACC 3SG-ends-PFV-FAC-GPAST
"The water put out the fire (but it's not necessarily relevant)."
Please, ignore the verb conjugation right now. I only included that here for the sake of documentation, we'll get there when we get there.
